# 30-Second Sit-to-Stand Test (30CST)

## 1. What it measures
Functional **lower-body (leg) strength and power endurance** — the number of full
stand-ups (sit→stand→sit, no arm assistance) completed in 30 seconds.
**Units: number of complete stands.**

## 2. Standard protocol
Straight-backed chair, seat height **43–44 cm (17″)**, placed against a wall.
Patient seated mid-chair, back straight, **arms crossed over the chest**, feet
flat. On "go," stand to full extension and return fully seated, repeated for
30 s; count complete stands (a stand more than halfway up at 30 s counts as one).
Source: Rikli & Jones Senior Fitness Test.

**Protocol caveat:** the young-adult validation study (Lein 2022) used a **45 cm**
chair and counted sit-to-stand-to-sit cycles — close but not identical, so its
numbers aren't perfectly interchangeable with the SFT norms.

The Rikli & Jones bands are derived from **~7,000 US community-dwelling older
adults** — the strongest available norms and the standard worldwide. The "normal
range" is the interquartile range; **below the 25th percentile = below average**
for age/sex, above the 75th = above average.

> ⚠ **Evidence gap — 45–59 cohort:** there is **no robust peer-reviewed
> sex/age-stratified norm table for ages 36–59**. The literature jumps from
> young-adult means (~33, Lein 2022, n=81) to the 60+ SFT norms. Do **not**
> fabricate intermediate bands. For 45–59 patients the defensible options are
> (a) interpret against the 60–64 norms as a conservative lower bound, or (b) use
> a different test with better middle-aged norms. Flag this to clinicians.

## 4. Interpretation thresholds
- **Below average:** score below the 25th-percentile value for the patient's
  age/sex band (table above).
- **Low functional ability:** **< 8 unassisted stands** associated with poorer
  functional ability (Rikli & Jones 1999).
- **Criterion cut-point for maintaining physical independence (Rikli & Jones
  2013):** falling below the age/sex threshold predicts future loss of
  independence. Confirmed values: **women 60–64 ≥ 15; men 60–64 ≥ 17**
  (thresholds decline with age).
  ⚠ Full 2013 cut-point table **not retrieved from open sources** (paywalled:
  *The Gerontologist* 53(2):255–267 / Senior Fitness Test Manual). Pull that
  table directly before relying on it.
- No single validated universal fall-risk cut-off for the 30CST alone (it's used
  within multifactorial screening).

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Strong criterion validity vs weight-adjusted leg-press: **r = 0.77** overall
  (men 0.78, women 0.71) (Jones et al. 1999).
- Lower / declining scores associated with reduced functional mobility,
  disability risk, and loss of independence (Rikli & Jones 1999/2013). Evidence
  is consistent and strong for older adults.

## 6. MDC / MCID
- **MCID ≈ 2 stands** (commonly cited); MCII **2.0–2.6** reps in hip OA
  (Wright 2011, SEM 1.27).
- **MDC ≈ 2.5 stands** for individuals in knee OA populations.
- **Reliability:** test–retest r = 0.89 (men 0.84, women 0.92) in
  community-dwelling elderly; interrater ICC 0.93–0.98 (Gill 2008).
- Practical read: **a change ≥ ~3 stands at reassessment is real**; smaller
  changes may be measurement noise.

# Timed Up and Go (TUG)

## 1. What it measures
**Functional mobility** — dynamic balance, gait, and transfer ability — as the
**time in seconds** to rise from a chair, walk 3 m, turn, return, and sit.

## 2. Standard protocol
Podsiadlo & Richardson (1991): standard **armchair (seat ~46 cm)**, on "go" the
patient stands, walks **3 metres** at a **comfortable, safe pace**, turns, walks
back, sits. Regular footwear, usual walking aid permitted, no physical
assistance. Timed from "buttocks-off" to "buttocks-on." One practice trial, then
time the trial (some protocols average two).

**Protocol note:** "comfortable pace" is the norm-referenced version; a
"fast/maximal pace" variant exists and produces faster times that are **not**
comparable to these norms.

## 3. Normative data table

| 80–99 | Combined | 11.3 | 95% CI 10.0–12.7 (pooled mean) | seconds | Bohannon 2006 |
| 60–64 | Men | 9.9 | SE ±0.1 (large n) | seconds | Ibrahim 2017 |
| 60–64 | Women | 10.7 | SE ±0.1 | seconds | Ibrahim 2017 |

| 70–74 | Men | 10.9 | SE ±0.1 | seconds | Ibrahim 2017 |

**Two honesty flags on this table:**
- **Population conflict:** Bohannon's pooled Western norms (60–69 ≈ 8.1 s) are
  notably **faster** than Ibrahim's Malaysian cohort (60–64 ≈ 10 s). Population/
  protocol differences are real — for these patients, **Bohannon (Western,
  pooled)** is the more applicable reference; Ibrahim is the best
  *sex-stratified* source but on an Asian sample.
- Bohannon's spread is the **95% CI of the pooled mean** (a population-average
  estimate), and Ibrahim's "±0.1" is almost certainly **standard error**, not SD
  (n is huge). Neither gives a true individual percentile reference range — treat
  these as "expected average time," not as cut-points.

> ⚠ **Evidence gap — 40–59:** robust sex/age-stratified TUG norms below 60 are
> sparse. A healthy-adult regression-equation study (Asian population, 21–85 yr)
> exists and could yield middle-aged estimates, but per-band values are
> unverified. Don't fabricate 40–59 rows; interpret younger patients against the
> 60–69 band as a lenient upper bound, or chase the regression study via
> OpenEvidence.

## 4. Interpretation thresholds
- **≥ 13.5 s** — high fall risk in community-dwelling older adults
  (Shumway-Cook 2000; 90% predictive). Most widely used cut-off.
- **≥ 12 s** — CDC **STEADI** screening threshold for increased fall risk.
- **≥ 9 s** — associated with risk of developing disability in community older
  adults (Makizako 2017).
- Most healthy older adults: **~6–11 s**; **> 20–30 s** indicates serious
  mobility impairment / likely need for assistance.
- Variant cut-offs: TUG-manual ≥ 14.5 s; TUG-cognitive ≥ 15.0 s.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Longer/declining TUG associated with **falls, mobility impairment, disability,
  and loss of independence**. For falls specifically, the evidence is better at
  **ruling in** high risk (pooled specificity 0.74) than ruling out (sensitivity
  0.31) — a fast TUG does **not** clear someone of fall risk.
- Predictive value strengthens in disease populations (post-hip-fracture ≥ 24 s;
  transtibial amputation ≥ 19 s).

## 6. MDC / MCID
- No single universal MCID; established **MDC values are population-specific**:
  Parkinson's **4.9 s**, Alzheimer's **4.1 s**, chronic stroke **2.9 s**.
- Reliability is excellent: interrater r ≈ 0.98–0.99.
- Practical read: in neurological/older populations, **a change of ~3 s (stroke)
  up to ~5 s (PD) at reassessment is meaningful**; smaller changes may be noise.
  A general healthy-older-adult MDC is less firmly established — flag rather than
  assume.

## 7. References

  PMID 1991946. *(original protocol)*

# Grip Strength (Handgrip Dynamometry)

## 1. What it measures
Maximal **isometric hand/forearm strength**, a validated proxy for **whole-body
strength** and a robust health/mortality marker. **Units: kilograms (kg).**

## 2. Standard protocol
Jamar (or Jamar+ digital) dynamometer, **ASHT/Southampton position**: seated,
shoulder adducted/neutral, elbow flexed **90°**, forearm and wrist neutral,
handle at **position 2**. Squeeze maximally; **3 trials per hand**, record the
**maximum** (or mean) — using max/mean gives higher reliability than a single
trial. Report the **dominant hand** or specify hand, as norms are hand- and
device-specific.

**Caveat:** different dynamometers (Jamar vs hydraulic vs Martin vigorimeter) and
handle positions are **not interchangeable** — match the norm set to your device.

## 3. Normative data table
Primary source = **Svinøy 2023 (Tromsø)** — Western, healthy adults (no NCD/OA),
with true percentiles. Values are maximum grip, kg.

This is the **best-fit table for the clinic**: Western population, healthy
adults, covers the full 40–79 range with real percentiles and proper SDs (unlike
TUG/30CST, **no 40–59 gap here**). The classic Bohannon 2006 Jamar meta-analysis
corroborates the age/sex pattern but its published table is in lb via secondary
sources, so Tromsø is kept as the primary.

## 4. Interpretation thresholds
- **Low strength / probable sarcopenia (EWGSOP2):** **< 27 kg men, < 16 kg
  women.** The dominant clinical cut-off; below it warrants further sarcopenia
  work-up.
- **Below average for age/sex:** below the **p25** value in the table.
- Note: at least five different sarcopenia cut-points exist in the literature;
  EWGSOP2 is the most widely used but not universal.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Grip strength is **one of the most robust physical predictors of all-cause
  mortality** in population studies. Low grip (dynapenia) independently predicts
  adverse events (e.g. adjusted HR ~2.3 in hospitalised older adults).
- Associated with frailty, disability, longer hospital stay, and poorer surgical
  outcomes. Evidence base is large and consistent.

## 6. MDC / MCID
- **MCID ≈ 5.0–6.5 kg** (anchor-based systematic review).
- **MDC ≈ 5–6 kg** for an individual (MDC90 ~6 kg; MDC95 4.7–6.2 kg);
  **SEM ~2.5 kg**.
- **Reliability:** ICC ≥ 0.95 (excellent), up to ~4 months apart.
- Practical read: **a change of ≥ ~6 kg at reassessment is real**; smaller shifts
  fall within measurement error.

# Joint Range of Motion (ROM)

## How to read this section (read first)
- **Units:** degrees. ROM is reported as the arc from neutral (0°) unless stated.
- **Two classes of reference value, and they are not equivalent:**
  1. **Age/sex-stratified norms** with confidence intervals — gold standard.
     Available for *some* peripheral motions only, from **Soucie 2011 (CDC)**
     (n=674, ages 2–69, healthy, **passive** goniometry) and, for the neck, from
     **Youdas 1992** (CROM, active).
  2. **AAOS / AMA consensus "standard adult" single values** — widely used in
     clinic, but **not age- or sex-stratified, no CI, and no healthy-population
     dispersion.** Used here only where no stratified data exist. AAOS and AMA
     frequently disagree (especially in the spine) — both are noted.
- **Age matters:** ROM declines with age. Judging an older patient against a
  single "standard adult" value over-calls restriction. **Prefer the Soucie/Youdas
  age band where it exists.**
- **Active vs passive:** Soucie used passive ROM; clinic AROM will read slightly
  lower. Match method to the norm set.
- **MDC (general goniometry):** intrarater minimal detectable change is commonly
  **~5°**, interrater larger (up to ~10° at some joints). Cervical CROM is tighter
  (3.6–6.5°). Practical read: **a change of ≥ ~5° (same rater) is likely real**;
  smaller is within measurement error. Spine end-range method-dependent values
  have wider error.
- **Interpretation principle:** below the age/sex reference (or below the
  AAOS/AMA standard where that's all we have) = reduced ROM / opportunity area;
  within = normal; the "Interpretation" column on the handout should name the
  functional consequence, not just the number.

> ⚠ **Evidence gaps flagged honestly:**
> - Age/sex-stratified norms exist for shoulder **flexion**, hip **flexion/
>   extension**, knee **flexion/extension**, ankle **dorsi/plantarflexion**
>   (Soucie), and **all cervical** motions (Youdas). For every other motion below
>   (shoulder abduction/rotation, hip abduction/adduction/rotation, ankle
>   inversion/eversion, wrist, and thoracic/lumbar) only **consensus standard
>   values** are available — do not present these as age-corrected.
> - **Thoracic-specific** ROM norms are sparse; most "thoracolumbar" values lump
>   thoracic + lumbar. The isolated thoracic-rotation test below has reliability
>   data but limited published normative means.

Isolated thoracic norms are **sparse**. Most clinical figures are *thoracolumbar*

  symmetry and the patient's own baseline as the primary reference, not a

- Thoracolumbar rotation (AAOS combined): **0–45° per side.**

> ⚠ Do not quote a precise population "normal" thoracic rotation in degrees — the
> evidence doesn't support one. Use symmetry and change-over-time.

# 4-Stage Balance Test

## 1. What it measures
**Static standing balance / postural stability**, as a graded fall-risk screen.
The test is fundamentally **pass/fail per stage** (seconds held, 10 s threshold);
the single-leg component also yields a **graded time in seconds**.

## 2. Standard protocol (CDC STEADI)
Four progressively harder positions, **eyes open, arms at sides, no assistive
device**, on a firm surface. Hold each for **10 seconds**; only progress to the
next if the current one is held. Stop when a position cannot be held for 10 s.
1. **Feet side-by-side**
2. **Semi-tandem** (instep of one foot beside the big toe of the other)
3. **Full tandem** (heel directly in front of the opposite toes)
4. **Single-leg stance**

**Scoring:** **Inability to hold the full-tandem stance for 10 s = increased fall
risk** (the key STEADI cut-off). A fail at stage 1, 2, or 3 (total < 30 s)
indicates high fall risk.

## 3. Normative data
The 4-Stage test is scored pass/fail, not against a mean. Its component stances
do have graded age norms:

**Single-leg stance, eyes open (mean seconds):**

| Age band | Sex | Mean (s) | Reference range / note | Units | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 40–49 | Combined | 41.9 | uncapped trial mean | seconds | Springer 2007 |
| 50–59 | Combined | 41.2 | | seconds | Springer 2007 |
| 60–69 | Combined | 32.1 | | seconds | Springer 2007 |
| 70–79 | Combined | 21.5 | | seconds | Springer 2007 |
| 80–99 | Combined | 9.4 | | seconds | Springer 2007 |
| 60–69 | Combined | 27.0 | meta-analysis | seconds | Bohannon 2006 |
| 70–79 | Combined | 17.2 | meta-analysis | seconds | Bohannon 2006 |
| 80–99 | Combined | 8.5 | meta-analysis | seconds | Bohannon 2006 |

Springer eyes-open protocol used the **best of trials, uncapped** (hence 40–59
≈ 41 s). Eyes-closed times are much shorter and decline faster with age. Springer
reported sex differences but the open-web extract didn't give a clean sex split —
flag.

> ⚠ **Two honesty flags:** (1) The **4-Stage test has no graded "norm"** — it's a
> 10 s/stage pass/fail screen; the seconds-norms above come from the *single-leg*
> and tandem literature, not the 4-Stage protocol itself. (2) Single-leg norms are
> **protocol-sensitive** (eyes open vs closed, arm position, trial cap) — match
> the patient's test conditions to the norm source.

## 4. Interpretation thresholds
- **Full tandem < 10 s** → increased fall risk (CDC STEADI). Primary cut-off.
- **Total 4-Stage time < 30 s** (fails before tandem) → high fall risk.
- **Single-leg stance < 5 s** → strongly associated with falls and injurious
  falls; near-inability is a robust adverse-outcome marker.
- **Cannot complete a 10 s single-leg stand (≥50 yr)** → associated with higher
  all-cause mortality (Araújo 2022).

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Reduced tandem/single-leg time predicts **future falls** (e.g. prospective
  6-month fall prediction in community-dwelling adults 60–89) and **injurious
  falls**; single-leg balance is also linked to **all-cause mortality**. Balance
  declines with age faster than many other measures, making it a sensitive early
  marker.

## 6. MDC / MCID
- No well-established MCID for the 4-Stage pass/fail test (it's categorical).
- Single-leg stance timing is reliable (ICC generally good–excellent), but a
  precise MDC in seconds varies widely by population and protocol and is **not
  firmly established** — interpret large changes (and side-to-side asymmetry)
  rather than small ones, and flag that a validated MDC is lacking here.

## 7. References
- **CDC STEADI** — *Assessment: The 4-Stage Balance Test.*
  *(protocol & fall-risk cut-off)*
- **Springer BA, Marin R, Cyhan T, et al. (2007).** Normative values for the
  unipedal stance test with eyes open and closed. *J Geriatr Phys Ther*
  30(1):8–15. PMID 19839175. *(USA, ages 18–99, eyes open/closed, age-stratified)*
- **Bohannon RW (2006).** Single limb stance times: a descriptive meta-analysis
  of data from individuals at least 60 years of age. *Top Geriatr Rehabil*
  22(1):70–77. *(meta-analysis, 22 studies, n=3,484, ≥60 yr)*
- **Araújo CG et al. (2022).** Successful 10-second one-legged stance performance
  predicts survival in middle-aged and older individuals. *Br J Sports Med*
  56(17):975–980. *(mortality association)*

# Mini-BESTest (Mini Balance Evaluation Systems Test)

## 1. What it measures
**Dynamic balance** across four sub-systems — anticipatory postural adjustments,
reactive postural control, sensory orientation, and dynamic gait. **14 items,
each scored 0–2; total 0–28** (higher = better). Units: points / 28.

## 2. Standard protocol
14 tasks performed in order: sit-to-stand, rise to toes, single-leg stance,
compensatory stepping (forward/backward/lateral), stance on firm surface eyes
open, on foam eyes closed, on an incline eyes closed, change in gait speed, walk
with head turns, walk with pivot turns, step over obstacles, and **TUG with a
dual (cognitive) task**. Equipment: firm floor, **foam pad**, incline/ramp,
armless chair, 3 m walkway, obstacle, stopwatch. Score the lower-performing side
where bilateral; sum to /28.

**Note:** an earlier version was scored /32 — confirm the /28 version is used
(current standard).

## 3. Normative data table
Age-stratified healthy norms — **O'Hoski 2014** (Canada, healthy adults ≥50).

| Age band | Sex | Mean (out of 28) | Reference note | Units | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 50–59 | Combined | 26.3 | SDs not in extract | points/28 | O'Hoski 2014 |
| 60–69 | Combined | 24.7 | | points/28 | O'Hoski 2014 |
| 70–79 | Combined | 21.0 | | points/28 | O'Hoski 2014 |
| 80–89 | Combined | 19.6 | | points/28 | O'Hoski 2014 |

Scores decline ~2–3 points per decade. **No published norm for under-50** in this
source (younger athletes sit near ceiling, ~27–28 — interpret a *drop* rather than
against a norm). Sex split not captured in the open extract — flag.

## 4. Interpretation thresholds
- **Below the age-band mean** (table) = below expected for age → balance
  impairment / opportunity area.
- **~≤ 20/28** associated with fall history; a cut-off of **16/28** gave ~85%
  sensitivity / 75% specificity for fallers in one cohort.
- Cut-offs are **population- and age-dependent** — prefer the O'Hoski age norm
  over a single fixed cut for 45–75 patients. Age-specific cut-offs (declining by
  decade) have been published but the open-web values were garbled, so they are
  not tabled here — verify before use.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Lower scores predict **falls** in community-dwelling older adults; the
  Mini-BESTest has shown **higher fall-identification accuracy than the Berg
  Balance Scale, TUG, or full BESTest** in head-to-head work, and has **less
  ceiling effect** than the Berg — useful for higher-functioning patients
  (relevant to the injured-athlete cohort).

## 6. MDC / MCID
- **MDC₉₅ ≈ 3.5 points** (Godi 2013); ~3.7 in a TKA cohort; SEM ~1.3–1.5.
- **MCID ≈ 4 points** (Parkinson's, substantial change); anchor-based estimates
  range **~2–4 points** depending on population/method.
- **Reliability:** interrater ICC 0.96, intrarater 0.92, Cronbach α 0.96
  (excellent).
- Practical read: **a change of ≥ ~4 points at reassessment is real and clinically
  meaningful**; smaller is within error.

## 7. References
- **Franchignoni F, Horak F, Godi M, Nardone A, Giordano A (2010).** Using
  psychometric techniques to improve the Balance Evaluation Systems Test: the
  mini-BESTest. *J Rehabil Med* 42(4):323–331. PMID 20461334. *(original
  instrument)*

  Canadian adults who are healthy and aged 50 years or older. *Phys Ther*
  94(3):334–342. PMID 24158091. *(Canada, healthy ≥50, age-stratified norms)*

# Blood Pressure (Resting)

## 1. What it measures
Arterial pressure at rest — **systolic/diastolic, mmHg**. A cardiovascular risk
and exercise-safety screen.

## 2. Standard protocol
Seated, back supported, legs uncrossed, arm at heart level, after **≥5 min
rest**; no caffeine/exercise/smoking in the preceding 30 min. Correct cuff size.
Average **≥2 readings** on separate occasions before drawing conclusions.
(Australian/Heart Foundation method.)

## 3. Category table (Australian)
| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | Diastolic (mmHg) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Optimal | < 120 | and < 80 | Heart Foundation / healthdirect |
| Normal | 120–129 | and/or 80–84 | Heart Foundation |
| High-normal | 130–139 | and/or 85–89 | Heart Foundation |
| Grade 1 (mild) HTN | 140–159 | and/or 90–99 | Heart Foundation |
| Grade 2 (moderate) HTN | 160–179 | and/or 100–109 | Heart Foundation |
| Grade 3 (severe) HTN | ≥ 180 | and/or ≥ 110 | Heart Foundation |
| Isolated systolic HTN | ≥ 140 | and < 90 | Heart Foundation |

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **Hypertension = ≥ 140/90 mmHg** (clinic). Healthy target generally **< 130/85**.
- **Relative exercise-screening flags (ACSM-style):** resting **≥ 180/110** → do
  not commence vigorous exercise; recheck/refer. Hypotension or symptoms
  (dizziness) also warrant caution.
- One high reading ≠ diagnosis — categories guide **GP referral**.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Higher BP → graded, continuous increase in stroke, ischaemic heart disease, and
  CV mortality risk (strong, consistent evidence).

## 6. Measurement note
- BP is variable visit-to-visit; white-coat effect is real. Use averaged,
  properly-rested readings; consider home/ambulatory confirmation before acting on
  borderline values.

## 7. References
- **National Heart Foundation of Australia** — Guideline for the diagnosis and
  management of hypertension in adults (2016, updated). PMID 27456450.
- **healthdirect (Australian Govt)** — Healthy blood pressure categories.

# Blood Glucose (Fasting & Random/Non-Fasting)

## 1. What it measures
Plasma glucose concentration — **mmol/L** — screening for impaired glucose
metabolism / diabetes.

## 2. Standard protocol
- **Fasting plasma glucose (FPG):** no caloric intake for **≥ 8 h**.
- **Random/casual:** any time, regardless of meals.
- **2-h OGTT:** 75 g glucose load, measure at 2 h.
Capillary fingerstick (glucometer) is a **screen**; venous plasma is needed for
diagnosis.

## 3. Threshold table (Australia / WHO criteria, non-pregnant)
| State | Fasting (mmol/L) | 2-h OGTT (mmol/L) | Random (mmol/L) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Normal | < 6.1 | < 7.8 | — | WHO / Diabetes Australia |
| Impaired fasting glucose (IFG) | 6.1–6.9 | — | — | WHO |
| Impaired glucose tolerance (IGT) | < 7.0 | 7.8–11.0 | — | WHO |
| Diabetes | **≥ 7.0** | **≥ 11.1** | **≥ 11.1** (with symptoms) | WHO / Diabetes Australia |
| HbA1c | — | — | diabetes ≥ 6.5% / 48 mmol/mol; pre-diabetes 6.0–6.4% | RACGP / Diabetes Australia |

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **Fasting:** normal < 6.1; **IFG (pre-diabetes) 6.1–6.9**; diabetes ≥ 7.0.
- **Random/non-fasting:** **≥ 11.1 with symptoms** is diagnostic; a high random
  value without symptoms needs fasting/OGTT/HbA1c confirmation. Random glucose is
  a weaker stand-alone screen — interpret loosely and confirm.
- **Exercise-safety flags:** hypoglycaemia **< 4.0 mmol/L** (treat before
  exercising); very high **≥ 14–15 mmol/L** (± ketones) → delay vigorous exercise
  (ADA caution).
- Diagnosis requires **repeat/lab confirmation** by the GP — the clinic screens.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- IFG/IGT predict progression to type 2 diabetes and elevated CV risk; chronic
  hyperglycaemia drives micro/macrovascular complications.

## 6. Measurement note
- Glucometer (capillary) values differ from venous plasma and vary with recent
  food, stress, and illness — never diagnose from a fingerstick.

## 7. References
- **Diabetes Australia / RACGP** — Management of type 2 diabetes (2024).
- **WHO** — Definition and diagnosis of diabetes mellitus (FPG ≥ 7.0, 2-h ≥ 11.1,
  IFG 6.1–6.9, IGT 7.8–11.0).
- **AIHW** — Diabetes pathology/diagnostic thresholds.

# Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

## 1. What it measures
Heart beats per minute at rest — **bpm** — an autonomic/cardiovascular fitness and
risk marker.

## 2. Standard protocol
Measured after **≥ 5 min seated/supine rest**, no recent caffeine/exercise; count
for 30–60 s (or HR monitor/ECG). Best taken on waking. Atrial fibrillation/
arrhythmia makes manual counts unreliable.

## 3. Normative data
| Group | RHR (bpm) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Healthy adults (normal range) | **60–100** | AHA |
| Typical healthy/fit middle band | ~55–85 | population data |
| Well-trained / endurance athletes | 40–60 (occasionally < 40) | AHA / sports cardiology |
| Men, 30s (middle 50%) | ~58–74 | cohort norms |
| Women, 30s (middle 50%) | ~62–78 | cohort norms |

Patterns: **women average ~4 bpm higher** than men; RHR rises only ~**1–2 bpm per
decade**; **lower (within normal) generally indicates better fitness.**

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **Normal 60–100 bpm.** **Bradycardia < 60** (often benign/athletic, but
  symptomatic brady → refer), **tachycardia > 100** at rest → investigate/refer.
- A **lower resting HR within normal range** is a favourable training-adaptation
  sign; a rising trend over time can flag overtraining, illness, or
  deconditioning.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Elevated RHR is independently associated with higher all-cause and
  cardiovascular mortality (large cohort evidence); each ~10 bpm increment carries
  measurable risk.

## 6. Measurement note
- Affected by caffeine, stress, hydration, fever, medications (β-blockers lower
  it). Trend matters more than a single reading; flag irregular rhythm for ECG.

## 7. References
- **American Heart Association** — Target/resting heart rate guidance (normal
  60–100; athletes lower).
- **Quer G et al. (2020).** Inter-/intraindividual variability in daily resting
  heart rate… cohort of 92,457 adults. PMC7001906. *(age/sex/seasonal patterns)*

# Neck Flexor Endurance Test (Deep Neck Flexor / DNF)

## 1. What it measures
**Endurance of the deep cervical flexors** (craniocervical flexors) — the chin-tuck
hold time. **Units: seconds.**

## 2. Standard protocol
Supine, knees bent. Patient performs a **craniocervical (chin-tuck) nod** and lifts
the head ~**2.5 cm** off the plinth, holding the chin tucked. Timing starts when
held; **stops when the chin-tuck is lost** (skin folds separate / head drops /
palpable superficial muscle dominance). Watch the chin-tuck, not just head height.

## 3. Normative data table
| Group | Sex | Mean (SD) | Units | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Healthy adults | Men | 38.9 (20.1) | seconds | Domenech 2011 |
| Healthy adults | Women | 29.4 (13.7) | seconds | Domenech 2011 |
| Adolescent/young (14–22) | Men | 35.6 (10.4) | seconds | Juul 2017 |
| Adolescent/young (14–22) | Women | 31.9 (8.5) | seconds | Juul 2017 |
| **Neck-pain population** | Combined | ~21.4 | seconds | Domenech 2011 |

Sex strongly influences score (men > women). Note the **wide SD** — interpret
against the sex mean and weight side/trend change over a single absolute value.
Age-stratified adult norms beyond the sex split are limited — flag.

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **Below the sex mean** = reduced deep neck flexor endurance.
- **~≤ 21 s** overlaps the neck-pain range and suggests cervical flexor
  dysfunction (a practical flag, not a strict diagnostic cut-off).

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Reduced DNF endurance is consistently associated with **neck pain, cervicogenic
  headache, and whiplash**; improves with targeted training, making it a useful
  re-test target.

## 6. MDC / reliability
- Intra/inter-rater reliability generally good (ICC ~0.7–0.9 across studies), but a
  single agreed MDC in seconds is **not firmly established** — interpret meaningful
  change conservatively and track the trend.

## 7. References
- **Domenech MA, Sizer PS, Dedrick GS, et al. (2011).** The deep neck flexor
  endurance test: normative data scores in healthy adults. *PM&R* 3(2):105–110.
  *(healthy adults, sex-stratified)*
- **Juul T et al. (2017).** Deep neck flexor endurance in the adolescent and young
  adult: normative data and associated attributes. PMID 28214618.

# Single-Leg Calf Raise (Standing Heel-Rise to Fatigue)

## 1. What it measures
**Plantarflexor (calf) strength-endurance** — single-leg heel raises to fatigue.
**Units: repetitions** (apps also derive height/work).

## 2. Standard protocol
Single-leg stance, **knee fully extended**, on a **10° incline**, **barefoot**,
cadence **30 raises/min (60 bpm metronome)**, full height each rep, fingertip
support for balance only. Continue **until no further full repetitions** are
possible. (Knee-bent variants test soleus and give different numbers — not
interchangeable.)

## 3. Normative data table
Best peer-reviewed general-population source = **Visser 2025 (Calf Raise App)**;
values are repetitions, **median (95% reference interval)**.

| Group | Sex | Median reps (95% RI) | Units | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| General adults (dominant leg) | Combined | 25 (13–50) | reps | Visser 2025 |
| General adults | Female | 24 (13–43) | reps | Visser 2025 |
| General adults | Male | 26 (14–53) | reps | Visser 2025 |
| Classic benchmark | Adult | ~25 | reps | Hébert-Losier 2017 |

Performance falls with **female sex, higher BMI, lower activity, and increasing
age**. Visser's sample skewed young/active (mean age 36, 88% active) — for older
patients use the **age-adjusted estimate** from the validated calculator
(achillestendontool.com/HRET) rather than the flat median.

> ⚠ Clean age-decade tables exist in Hébert-Losier 2017 (n=566, 20–81 yr) via
> regression equations, but the per-decade numbers couldn't be extracted from open
> sources — use their online calculator for an age/sex-adjusted target, and flag
> that the flat "~25" benchmark over-expects in older adults.

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **Below the age/sex-adjusted expected reps** = reduced calf endurance.
- **Side-to-side asymmetry** (e.g. >10–15% deficit vs the uninvolved leg) is a key
  flag, especially post Achilles/calf injury and for return-to-sport.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Low calf endurance / asymmetry is associated with Achilles tendinopathy, calf
  injury, and impaired push-off; a standard return-to-running/sport criterion.

## 6. MDC / reliability
- Test–retest reliability good (ICC ~0.7–0.9); app-based scoring improves
  consistency. A precise population MDC varies by method — interpret asymmetry and
  clear changes over small ones.

## 7. References
- **Visser TSOS, O'Neill S, Hébert-Losier K, et al. (2025).** Normative values for
  calf muscle strength-endurance assessed with the Calf Raise Application: a large

# Single-Leg Rise Test (Single-Leg Sit-to-Stand)

## 1. What it measures
**Unilateral lower-limb (quadriceps) strength-endurance and control** —
sit-to-stand on one leg. **Units: repetitions** (30-s count) or **time for 5 reps**,
depending on protocol.

## 2. Standard protocol
Seated on a standard/recorded-height chair, one foot on the floor, the other leg
held off the ground; stand fully and sit, **one leg only**, arms typically crossed.
Two common formats: **(a) max reps in 30 s**, or **(b) time to complete 5 reps**.
Test both sides. Protocols differ in chair height and arm position — **record the
method**, as it changes the score.

## 3. Normative data
> ⚠ **Honesty flag — weak normative base.** There is **no robust, age/sex-stratified
> peer-reviewed norm table** for the single-leg sit-to-stand. The evidence is
> heterogeneous (different rep counts/protocols) and mostly young/clinical samples.
> Use the patient's **own baseline and side-to-side symmetry** as the primary
> reference.

Available reference points (use cautiously, not as validated norms):

| Reference point | Value | Source / caveat |
|---|---|---|
| Knee-health benchmark (ACLR, 3-yr) | **> 22 reps / 30 s** linked to better knee health | single cohort, ACLR-specific |
| Field-guidance bands (30 s) | Strong ≥15 · Good 10–14 · Moderate 6–9 · Developing 1–5 | practitioner heuristic, **not validated norms** |

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **Side-to-side asymmetry** is the most defensible interpretation (involved vs
  uninvolved limb).
- Treat absolute counts as a tracked baseline, not a population-referenced norm.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Reflects unilateral quadriceps strength/endurance; used in **ACL and knee rehab**
  progression and return-to-sport decision-making.

## 6. MDC / reliability
- Two newer single-leg STS variants showed acceptable inter-rater and test–retest
  reliability (Waldhelm 2020), but a validated MDC and population norms are lacking
  — flag.

## 7. References
- **Waldhelm A et al. (2020).** Inter-rater and test–retest reliability of two new
  single-leg sit-to-stand tests. *Int J Sports Phys Ther.* *(reliability)*
- Single-leg STS validation for knee-extensor strength/endurance (young adults) —
  construct validity only, not norms.

# Waist Circumference

## 1. What it measures
**Central (abdominal/visceral) adiposity** — a cardiometabolic risk marker.
**Units: cm.**

## 2. Standard protocol
Standing, feet together, at end of normal expiration; tape horizontal, snug not
compressing. WHO landmark: **midpoint between the lowest rib and the iliac crest**
(some protocols use the umbilicus or narrowest point — record the landmark, as it
shifts the value).

## 3. Threshold table (sex- and ethnicity-specific)
| Population | Increased risk | Substantially increased risk | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Men (Europid/Australian) | ≥ 94 cm | ≥ 102 cm | NHMRC / IDF / WHO |
| Women (Europid/Australian) | ≥ 80 cm | ≥ 88 cm | NHMRC / IDF / WHO |
| Men (South/East Asian) | ≥ 90 cm | — | IDF |
| Women (South/East Asian) | ≥ 80 cm | — | IDF |

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- Two-tier: **≥94/≥80 (M/F) = increased**, **≥102/≥88 = substantially increased**
  cardiometabolic risk (Europid). Use **lower Asian cut-offs** where relevant.
- Complements BMI; better than BMI alone for visceral risk. Screen → lifestyle
  advice / GP, not a diagnosis.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Strong, graded association with type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and
  all-cause mortality — robust evidence.

## 6. Measurement note
- Landmark and end-expiration matter; same rater/landmark for re-tests. A
  reduction of a few cm is clinically meaningful for risk.

## 7. References
- **WHO (2008/2011).** Waist circumference and waist–hip ratio: report of a WHO
  expert consultation.
- **Klein S et al. (2007).** Waist circumference and cardiometabolic risk:
  consensus statement. *Diabetes Care* 30(6):1647–1652.
- **NHMRC** Australian clinical practice guidelines for overweight/obesity
  (≥94/≥80, ≥102/≥88).

# Muscle-Length & Postural Assessments (qualitative)

> **Read first — why these are grouped and caveated.** These tests are **clinically
> useful but largely qualitative**. With the partial exception of pectoralis-minor
> length and the (pelvis-controlled) modified Thomas test, they have **limited or
> no population normative data**, are often scored **pass/fail**, and several have
> **poor inter-rater reliability**. For these, the defensible interpretation is
> **side-to-side asymmetry and the patient's own change over time**, not a
> population norm. The handout interpretation should describe findings as
> "tight / restricted / symmetrical," not pin a number to a norm that doesn't exist.

## Pectoralis Minor Length
- **Measures:** resting pec minor length / tightness (scapular anterior tilt driver).
- **Protocol:** supine; tape from inferomedial coracoid process to the caudal edge
  of the 4th rib at the sternum. **PMI = (length ÷ height) × 100.**
- **Normative values:** PML — **men 14.59 ± 1.61 cm, women 12.95 ± 1.42 cm**;
  PMI — **men 8.54 ± 0.88, women 8.22 ± 0.90** (asymptomatic adults). A *lower*
  PML/PMI = shorter/tighter pec minor.
- **Reliability:** intra-rater good–excellent (ICC 0.76–0.93); **inter-rater only
  moderate (0.64–0.72)** — keep the same rater for re-tests.
- **Source:** normative pilot (asymptomatic adults); Struyf et al. (reliability).

## Modified Thomas Test (hip flexor length)
- **Measures:** iliopsoas/rectus femoris length via peak hip-extension angle.
- **Protocol:** supine at table edge, contralateral knee hugged to flatten lumbar
  spine; tested thigh lowers. Measure hip-extension angle (inclinometer/
  goniometer). **Thigh resting at/below horizontal ≈ normal hip-flexor length**
  (common pass/fail read).
- **Normative value:** healthy hip extension **~5.4° ± 9.7°** — note the **very
  wide SD**.
- **Critical caveat:** **valid only if pelvic tilt is controlled** (ICC ~0.85–0.91
  controlled; **poor validity/reliability uncontrolled**). Without pelvis control,
  treat as pass/fail + symmetry only.
- **Source:** modified-Thomas validity/reliability studies (PeerJ 2016;
  goniometric reliability 2024).

## Latissimus Dorsi Length
- **Measures:** lat tightness limiting overhead shoulder flexion.
- **Protocol:** supine, knees bent, lumbar flattened (posterior pelvic tilt); flex
  shoulders with elbows extended. **Positive (tight) = unable to reach 180°
  shoulder flexion** with the back flat.
- **Normative data:** **none usable.** Scored pass/fail. Reliability
  **poor–moderate** (intra-rater ICC 0.55–0.76; inter-rater 0.33). Not suitable
  for research-grade tracking.
- **Interpretation:** symmetry + own change only; flag low reliability.

## Thoracic Extension Test
- **Measures:** thoracic extension mobility (often relevant to overhead/posture).
- **Protocol:** varies (seated, supine over a fulcrum/foam roller, wall).
  Inclinometer or visual.
- **Normative data:** **no universal norms** — thoracic extension can't be cleanly
  isolated from lumbar/combined motion, and reported values are **"representative,
  not normative."** (By contrast, thoracic *rotation* has reliable methods:
  ICC 0.85–0.94, SEM 1.0–2.3°, MDC 2.8–6.3°.)
- **Interpretation:** track the patient's own change and symmetry; don't quote a
  population "normal."

## Serratus Anterior Length Assessment
- **Honest flag:** there is **no validated "length" test or norms** for serratus
  anterior. It's assessed functionally by **strength** (hand-held dynamometry —
  **poor inter-rater reliability, ICC 0.28**; isolated best in seated 90° scaption)
  or **ultrasound thickness** (reliable), and by **scapular position/winging**, not
  length.
- **Interpretation:** report observationally (e.g. scapular winging, dyskinesis,
  protraction strength asymmetry); avoid implying a measured length norm.

## References (qualitative section)
- Pectoralis minor: resting-length normative pilot (asymptomatic adults);
  **Struyf F et al.** reliability of pec minor resting length.
- **Modified Thomas:** *The modified Thomas test is not a valid measure of hip
  extension unless pelvic tilt is controlled.* PeerJ 2016;4:e2325. + goniometric
  reliability (2024).
- **Latissimus dorsi:** Inter-/intra-rater reliability of a LD length technique.
  PMID 30135913.
- **Thoracic:** Johnson KD et al. Thoracic spine extension mobility in young
  adults: influence of position. *JOSPT* 2011;41(4). + thoracic rotation
  reliability (PMC3418115).
- **Serratus anterior:** Hand-held dynamometry validity/reliability (BMC
  Musculoskelet Disord 2019); ultrasound thickness reliability (2013).

# 6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT)

## 1. What it measures
**Submaximal aerobic capacity / functional walking endurance** — distance walked
in 6 minutes. **Units: metres.**

## 2. Standard protocol
ATS standard: flat, straight **30 m corridor**, cones at each end, walk **as far as
possible in 6 min** (may slow/stop/rest but keep timing). Standardised phrasing and
**fixed encouragement only** at set intervals. Record distance, plus pre/post HR,
SpO₂, RPE, and any stops. A practice walk affects results — note if first attempt.

## 3. Normative data / reference equations
No single flat "normal"; distance depends on age, sex, height, weight. Use the
**Enright sex-specific reference equations** (healthy adults 40–80):
- **Men:** 6MWD = (7.57 × height_cm) − (5.02 × age) − (1.76 × weight_kg) − 309
- **Women:** 6MWD = (2.11 × height_cm) − (2.29 × weight_kg) − (5.78 × age) + 667

| Group | Median distance (m) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Healthy men (40–80) | ~576 | Enright 1998 |
| Healthy women (40–80) | ~494 | Enright 1998 |
| Healthy adults (typical range) | ~400–700 | general population |

> ⚠ ATS/ERS caution: predicted values **vary substantially between equations** —
> apply one equation consistently and prefer a local/population-matched one where
> available. Report the patient's distance as **% of predicted**.

### ⚠ Course-length correction — 10 m track (clinic-specific)
The Enright equations and all norms above assume the **ATS 30 m course**. A
shorter track forces more turns (each turn costs time/distance), so a 10 m track
yields a **systematically shorter** 6MWD — you will under-read against 30 m norms
if you ignore this.

- **10 m vs 30 m:** ~**49.5 m shorter** on the 10 m course (COPD crossover study);
  a 10 m result explains only ~**73%** of the 30 m distance. Turns on a 10 m track
  are ~3× those on 30 m, and more turns → bigger negative gap.
- **20 m vs 30 m:** ~**22 m** difference (<10%) — a closer substitute if you can
  extend the track.
- **No validated healthy-adult reference equation exists for a 10 m track** — do
  not back-calculate a fake "% predicted." 

**How to use the 10 m track defensibly:**
1. **Standardise it** — same 10 m course, layout, and instructions for every test.
2. **Primary interpretation = the patient's own serial change** on that track
   (intra-individual comparison is valid and is where the MCID applies).
3. If comparing to the Enright 30 m prediction, **expect a ~30–50 m negative bias**
   and interpret leniently — a "low % predicted" on a 10 m track may be the track,
   not the patient.
4. **Record the course length on every result** so re-tests are comparable.

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- Compare measured distance to **% predicted** (Enright); lower = reduced aerobic/
  functional capacity.
- **MCID ≈ 14–30.5 m** (commonly ~30 m) — improvement beyond this is clinically
  meaningful at reassessment.
- Desaturation (SpO₂ drop ≥4% or <88%) during the walk is a referral flag.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Predicts morbidity/mortality in cardiopulmonary disease (COPD, heart failure,
  PAH) and tracks functional capacity in deconditioned/older adults. Robust
  evidence in clinical populations.

## 6. MDC / MCID
- **MCID 14–30.5 m**; reliability excellent (ICC typically >0.90). Trend and
  % predicted matter more than a single absolute figure.

## 7. References
- **Enright PL, Sherrill DL (1998).** Reference equations for the six-minute walk
  in healthy adults. *Am J Respir Crit Care Med* 158(5):1384–1387. PMID 9817683.
  *(USA, n=290, ages 40–80)*
- **ATS Committee (2002).** ATS statement: guidelines for the six-minute walk test.
  *Am J Respir Crit Care Med* 166:111–117. *(protocol)*

# Gait Speed (Comfortable Walking Speed)

## 1. What it measures
**Usual walking speed** — a powerful global indicator of function, frailty, and
survival (the "sixth vital sign"). **Units: m/s.**

## 2. Standard protocol
Time the patient walking at their **comfortable usual pace** over a measured
**central 3–10 m**, with **~2 m acceleration and 2 m deceleration** zones excluded
from timing. Two trials, averaged. Usual footwear, customary walking aid permitted.
(A separate **maximum**-speed version exists — don't mix the two.)

## 3. Normative data table
Comfortable gait speed, **mean (SD)** — Bohannon (reference data).

| Age band | Sex | Mean m/s (SD) | Units | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 50–59 | Women | 1.10 (0.24) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |
| 50–59 | Men | 1.12 (0.24) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |
| 60–69 | Women | 0.99 (0.24) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |
| 60–69 | Men | 1.03 (0.23) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |
| 70–79 | Women | 0.92 (0.24) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |
| 70–79 | Men | 0.95 (0.24) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |
| 80+ | Women | 0.76 (0.22) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |
| 80+ | Men | 0.84 (0.24) | m/s | Bohannon 2008 |

(Younger adults 20–49 average ~1.3–1.45 m/s; female speed declines from ~30 yr,
male from ~50 yr.)

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **≥ 1.1 m/s** high-functioning; **0.7–1.0 m/s** typical/mid; **< 0.7 m/s** low —
  predicts falls, hospitalisation, adverse events; **< 0.6 m/s** predicts further
  functional decline.
- **< 0.8 m/s** common community-ambulation / slow-gait threshold; **~1.0 m/s** ≈
  cut for community mobility and a recognised frailty marker.

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Slower gait speed independently predicts **mortality, falls, hospitalisation,
  disability, and cognitive decline** — one of the most robust functional
  biomarkers in the literature.

## 6. MDC / MCID
- **MDC small** (~0.08–0.11 m/s in hip-fracture/COPD); a **substantial meaningful
  change ≈ 0.10 m/s**, small meaningful change ≈ 0.05 m/s.
- Reliability excellent (ICC 0.90–0.96).

## 7. References
- **Bohannon RW, Williams Andrews A (2011).** Normal walking speed: a descriptive
  meta-analysis. *Physiotherapy* 97(3):182–189. *(pooled age/sex norms)*
- **Bohannon RW (1997/2008).** Comfortable and maximum walking speed of adults
  20–79 yr: reference values and determinants. *Age Ageing* 26(1):15–19.
  PMID 9143432.

# Five Times Sit-to-Stand (5×STS / FTSST)

## 1. What it measures
**Lower-limb (mainly quadriceps) strength, transfer ability, and dynamic balance**
— time to complete 5 sit-to-stand cycles. **Units: seconds (lower = better).**
Complements the 30CST (this is a *speed-to-complete-5* test, not a *reps-in-30 s*
test).

## 2. Standard protocol
Standard chair **43–45 cm (17–18″)** with backrest, against a wall. Patient sits,
**arms folded across chest**, back against chair. "Stand up and sit down 5 times as
fast as you can." Timing from "Go" to the **buttocks touching on the 5th sit**. No
arm use.

## 3. Normative data table
Mean time, Bohannon reference data.

| Age band | Sex | Mean time (s) | Units | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 19–49 | Combined | 6.2 | seconds | Bohannon 2007 |
| 50–59 | Combined | 7.1 | seconds | Bohannon 2007 |
| 60–69 | Combined | 11.4 | seconds | Bohannon 2006/2007 |
| 70–79 | Combined | 12.6 | seconds | Bohannon 2006/2007 |
| 80–89 | Combined | 14.8 | seconds | Bohannon 2006/2007 |

Sex-split norms are less consistently reported — flag; the values above are
combined/age-based.

## 4. Interpretation / thresholds
- **Above the age mean** (slower) = reduced lower-limb strength/function.
- **≥ 12 s** → flag for further fall-risk assessment (Tiedemann 2008).
- **> 15 s** → predicts recurrent falls in older adults (Buatois 2010).
- Population-specific: Parkinson's **> 16 s** = fall risk (Duncan 2011); vestibular
  **>13–14 s** (Whitney 2005).

## 5. Clinical correlations
- Slower 5×STS associated with **falls, frailty, lower-limb weakness, and reduced
  mobility**; widely used and responsive in rehab.

## 6. MDC / MCID
- Reliability excellent across populations (ICC 0.91–0.99; SEM ~0.6 s in PD).
- A precise general-population MDC isn't firmly standardised; in clinical groups a
  change of **~2–2.5 s** is generally considered meaningful — interpret clear
  changes over small ones.

## 7. References
- **Bohannon RW (2006).** Reference values for the five-repetition sit-to-stand
  test: a descriptive meta-analysis of data from elders. *Percept Mot Skills*
  103(1):215–222. PMID 17037663. *(elders ≥60)*
- **Bohannon RW (2007).** Reference values extended to younger adult age bands.
- **Tiedemann A et al. (2008)**; **Buatois S et al. (2010)**; **Duncan RP et al.
  (2011)** *(fall-risk cut-offs)*.
